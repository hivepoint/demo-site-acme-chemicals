    <h1>Understanding GHS and HazCom 2012</h1>
    <p class="lead">The Globally Harmonized System of Classification and Labelling of Chemicals transformed chemical safety communication worldwide. Here's a practical guide to GHS and OSHA HazCom 2012 compliance for employers and employees.</p>
  </div>
</div>

<div class="blog-content">
  <img src="https://images.unsplash.com/photo-1434626881859-194d67b2b86f?w=860&h=480&fit=crop" alt="Chemical hazard labels and safety documentation">

  <p>Before the Globally Harmonized System (GHS), the same chemical could have different hazard classifications, different label formats, and different Safety Data Sheets in different countries — creating confusion and genuine safety risks for workers and emergency responders. GHS was developed under the auspices of the United Nations to bring consistency to chemical hazard communication globally.</p>

  <p>In the US, OSHA's Hazard Communication Standard (HazCom 2012) adopted GHS, replacing the previous MSDS system with 16-section Safety Data Sheets and standardized labels. Understanding these requirements is essential for anyone who works with hazardous chemicals.</p>

  <h2>GHS Hazard Classification</h2>

  <p>GHS classifies chemicals across two main hazard categories, each with specific sub-classes:</p>

  <h3>Physical Hazards</h3>
  <ul>
    <li>Flammable liquids (Categories 1–4 based on flash point and boiling point)</li>
    <li>Flammable gases, aerosols, and solids</li>
    <li>Oxidizing liquids and solids</li>
    <li>Explosives and self-reactive substances</li>
    <li>Compressed gases</li>
    <li>Organic peroxides</li>
    <li>Corrosive to metals</li>
  </ul>

  <h3>Health Hazards</h3>
  <ul>
    <li>Acute toxicity (Categories 1–5 by oral, dermal, and inhalation routes)</li>
    <li>Skin corrosion/irritation</li>
    <li>Serious eye damage/irritation</li>
    <li>Respiratory or skin sensitization</li>
    <li>Germ cell mutagenicity</li>
    <li>Carcinogenicity</li>
    <li>Reproductive toxicity</li>
    <li>STOT — specific target organ toxicity (single and repeated exposure)</li>
    <li>Aspiration hazard</li>
  </ul>

  <blockquote>The most important thing to understand about GHS hazard categories is the numbering: Category 1 is always the MOST hazardous, and higher numbers represent decreasing hazard. A flammable liquid Category 1 (flash point &lt;23°C, boiling point &lt;35°C) is more hazardous than Category 4 (flash point 60–93°C).</blockquote>

  <h2>The 16-Section SDS Structure</h2>

  <p>GHS SDS have a standardized 16-section structure. The sections you'll use most frequently in emergency situations:</p>

  <ul>
    <li><strong>Section 1:</strong> Chemical identity and supplier contact (including emergency phone number)</li>
    <li><strong>Section 2:</strong> Hazard identification — GHS classification, signal word, hazard and precautionary statements</li>
    <li><strong>Section 4:</strong> First-aid measures — what to do if someone is exposed</li>
    <li><strong>Section 5:</strong> Firefighting measures — suitable extinguishing media, special hazards</li>
    <li><strong>Section 6:</strong> Accidental release measures — spill containment procedures</li>
    <li><strong>Section 8:</strong> Exposure controls/PPE — OELs and required protection</li>
    <li><strong>Section 14:</strong> Transport information — UN number and shipping classification</li>
  </ul>

  <h2>GHS Label Elements</h2>

  <p>GHS labels on hazardous chemical containers must include six elements:</p>

  <ol>
    <li><strong>Product identifier:</strong> Chemical name or trade name</li>
    <li><strong>Signal word:</strong> Either "Danger" (more severe hazards) or "Warning" (less severe)</li>
    <li><strong>Hazard statements:</strong> Standardized phrases describing the hazards (e.g., "H225 Highly flammable liquid and vapor")</li>
    <li><strong>Precautionary statements:</strong> Standardized phrases describing protective measures (P codes)</li>
    <li><strong>Pictograms:</strong> The nine GHS pictograms (flame, skull and crossbones, exclamation mark, etc.) in a red-bordered diamond</li>
    <li><strong>Supplier information:</strong> Name, address, and emergency phone number</li>
  </ol>

  <h2>HazCom 2012 Employer Obligations</h2>

  <p>Under OSHA HazCom 2012, employers who use hazardous chemicals must:</p>

  <ul>
    <li>Maintain a written hazard communication program</li>
    <li>Maintain a chemical inventory and SDS for all hazardous chemicals in the workplace</li>
    <li>Ensure SDS are accessible to employees at all times during their shift</li>
    <li>Ensure all containers are properly labeled</li>
    <li>Train employees on GHS and the hazards of the specific chemicals they work with</li>
  </ul>

  <p>The training requirement is the most commonly cited deficiency in OSHA chemical standard inspections. Generic "GHS awareness" training is not sufficient — employees must understand the specific hazards of the chemicals they use and the protective measures required for their specific tasks.</p>
